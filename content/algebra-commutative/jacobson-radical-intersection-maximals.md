---
title: "Jacobson Radical as Intersection of Maximal Ideals"
description: "In a commutative ring, the Jacobson radical equals the intersection of all maximal ideals."
---

## Statement

Let \(R\) be a commutative ring. The {{< knowl id="jacobson-radical" section="algebra-rings" text="Jacobson radical" >}} \(J(R)\) is given by
\[
J(R)=\bigcap_{\mathfrak m \text{ maximal}} \mathfrak m,
\]
the intersection of all {{< knowl id="maximal-ideal" section="algebra-rings" text="maximal ideals" >}} of \(R\).

A useful equivalent characterization is:
\[
x\in J(R) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad 1-ax \text{ is a unit for every } a\in R,
\]
where “unit” means {{< knowl id="unit" section="algebra-rings" text="invertible element" >}}.

In particular, if \(R\) is a {{< knowl id="local-ring" text="local ring" >}} with maximal ideal \(\mathfrak m\), then \(J(R)=\mathfrak m\).

(Existence of maximal ideals in nonzero commutative rings uses {{< knowl id="existence-of-maximal-ideals" section="algebra-rings" text="existence of maximal ideals" >}}.)

## Examples

1. **Fields.**  
   If \(R=k\) is a field, the only maximal ideal is \((0)\). Hence
   \[
   J(k)=(0).
   \]

2. **A local ring.**  
   Let \(R=k[x]/(x^2)\). This is local with maximal ideal \(\mathfrak m=(\bar x)\). Therefore
   \[
   J(R)=\mathfrak m=(\bar x).
   \]

3. **The integers.**  
   In \(R=\mathbb Z\), the maximal ideals are \((p)\) for primes \(p\). Their intersection is \((0)\), so
   \[
   J(\mathbb Z)=(0).
   \]
   (Similarly, in \(\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z\), \(J(\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)\) is the ideal generated by the product of the distinct primes dividing \(n\).)
